The CI ±0.22 means: if experts find that 50% of the fail-tier images are actually good (false negatives), the
  confidence interval would be 28%–72%. That's a wide range — you can't tell if it's 30% or 70% false negatives. With
   more samples (n=20 instead of 13), it narrows to 28%–72% → still wide but better.

  But you're right — this statistical framing is overthinking it. You're not running a hypothesis test. The goal is:

  1. Get signal: Do experts agree with the judge? Where do they disagree? Which failure modes matter to experts vs
  the judge?
  2. Calibrate: Is the morph≥4.0 threshold right? Should borderline images pass? Are some hard_fails actually usable?
  3. Scale: Use expert labels to potentially train a lightweight filter or adjust the judge prompt/threshold

  For these goals, what matters is:
  - Seeing enough of each tier to form qualitative patterns (not statistical precision)
  - Covering the interesting boundary — borderline and soft_fail are the most informative tiers
  - Not wasting expert time on obvious cases (too many strict_pass that are clearly fine, or too many hard_fail that
  are clearly bad)
